Digital Content-Psychology-Mental Health Model

Co-lead Researcher & Clinical Validation Specialist

üìã Research Summary

Victoria co-leads this comprehensive systematic review examining the psychological mechanisms that link different digital content modalities (text, image, video) to adolescent anxiety and depression. This research addresses a critical gap in understanding how content-specific effects impact mental health outcomes.

üîç Key Findings

  • Video Platform Effects: Short-form video platforms show strongest associations - depression d=0.42, anxiety d=0.38, body image concerns d=0.55
  • Content Modality Differences: Text-based platforms show significantly smaller effects (depression d=0.21, anxiety d=0.19)
  • Clinical Significance: Video platform users 1.84x more likely to meet depression criteria, 1.93x for anxiety
  • Psychological Mechanisms: Enhanced parasocial relationships, intensified social comparison, algorithm-driven attention disruption

üìà Research Scope

  • Studies Analyzed: 47 peer-reviewed studies
  • Total Participants: 156,789 adolescents
  • Platforms Examined: TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, Twitter, Facebook, Snapchat
  • Analysis Period: 2020-2024 research publications

95% Complete - Final manuscript preparation for submission

Video Content Psychology Research

Statistical Analysis Specialist

üìã Research Summary

Victoria leads the statistical analysis of the most comprehensive study to date on short-form video platform psychology. This research investigates parasocial relationships, algorithm-driven content effects, and platform-specific impacts on adolescent mental health across TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts.

üîç Key Psychological Mechanisms

  • Parasocial Relationships: Enhanced emotional connections with content creators (d=0.35)
  • Social Comparison: Intensified upward comparison processes (d=0.48)
  • Algorithm Effects: Attention disruption and dopamine cycling patterns (d=0.33)
  • Content Personalization: Filter bubble effects on mood and self-perception
  • Engagement Patterns: Infinite scroll and micro-dopamine reward systems

üìà Research Methodology

  • Total Participants: 156,789 adolescents across 47 studies
  • Statistical Methods: Random-effects meta-analysis using DerSimonian-Laird method
  • Quality Assessment: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale for study evaluation
  • Platform Coverage: TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, Snapchat

üìä Current Status

90% Complete - Final manuscript submitted for peer review
